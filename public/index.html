      Module 01

      Role Definition.
      What problem are you actually solving?

      Most job descriptions describe the last person who held the role, not the problem the next person needs to solve. This module forces the clarity most searches skip.

      The Business Reality Check

      Define what is breaking or at risk because this seat is empty or wrong, and what the business needs to be true in 12 months.

      The Failure Autopsy

      Understand what happened with the last leader, or which capability gap created the need for a new role.

      The Stakeholder Alignment Test

      Ask every key stakeholder separately what year-one success looks like, then resolve material differences.

      The Internal Environment Scan

      Identify the behaviors actually rewarded internally and the leadership styles that struggle in this culture.

      Do not start with the leader. Start with the problem.
      Ask yourself the following four questions...

      What happens to the business if this role stays open another six months?
      What kind of leader has failed here, and what specifically went wrong?
      What does year-one success look like in measurable terms, not adjectives?
      Where are you willing to flex on requirements, and where are you not?

      Module 02

      My Outcome Scorecard.
      Rate what actually matters.

      Rate each outcome from 1, low importance, to 5, non-negotiable. Meaning comes from the distribution, not the total alone: your 5s become interview anchors, while low-rated outcomes should not distract the hiring team.

      Rating total, not a pass/fail score: 0 / 35

      Complete the scorecard to reveal your priorities.

      For the strongest insight, ask each stakeholder to complete this independently, then compare where ratings differ by two or more points.

      Module 03

      Capability Matrix.
      What is actually non-negotiable?

      The expensive mistake is treating nice-to-haves as must-haves. Customize these columns to the specific role before interviewing.

      Must-Have on Day One
      Can Be Developed
      Non-Negotiable Disqualifiers

      Track record closing complex B2B sales at the required deal size

      Specific product or technical knowledge

      Has never led and developed a sales team

      Experience managing and developing a quota-carrying team

      Proficiency with the current CRM and sales stack

      Requires mature infrastructure to succeed in a builder role

      Builder mentality and comfort without heavy infrastructure

      Familiarity with a specific customer segment or geography

      Cannot explain how they hire, coach, and develop reps

      Communication skills strong enough to represent the brand

      Internal workflows unique to the organization

      Cannot discuss failures, lessons, and changed behavior

      If stakeholders disagree about which column an item belongs in, resolve that disagreement before interviewing candidates.

      Module 04

      Interview Alignment.
      Turn clarity into a consistent decision.

      The diagnostic identifies risk. This module prevents that risk from returning during interviews by assigning evidence, ownership, and decision rules before candidates enter the process.

      Assign Evidence Owners

      Give each interviewer specific outcomes and capabilities to validate instead of asking everyone to judge everything.

      Define Proof Before Interviews

      Agree on what strong, weak, and disqualifying evidence looks like for every non-negotiable criterion.

      Protect Independent Judgment

      Require written ratings before the debrief so the loudest stakeholder does not reshape everyone else's view.

      Set the Decision Rule

      Decide which gaps are coachable, which require more evidence, and which automatically stop the process.

      Ask yourself the following four questions...

      Who owns validation of each year-one outcome?
      What evidence would cause us to change our mind about a candidate?
      Which criteria require unanimous agreement?
      How will we capture candidate feedback and process friction?

      The real problem

      I am not here to fill seats. I am here to find truth.

      Most hiring processes are built around speed and appearances. Companies write job descriptions describing the last person who held the role. Candidates rehearse answers. Interviewers mistake credentials for capability. Then everyone wonders why the hire did not work out.

      I work differently. I use intentional listening, radical curiosity, and repeated questioning to get beneath the surface with clients, candidates, and the role itself.

      That is not a sales pitch. It is a methodology, and it produces a fundamentally different result than a transactional process.
